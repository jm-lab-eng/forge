DRACO

In-Service Crude Oil Tank Cleaning Robot

A multi-year engineering program focused on developing a robotic system capable of cleaning crude oil storage tanks while in service, improving safety and reducing operational downtime.

Project Overview

Crude oil storage tanks accumulate sludge over time, reducing storage capacity and creating operational challenges. Conventional tank cleaning methods often require shutdown of the tank and manual entry into hazardous environments.

The DRACO project focused on developing a robotic system capable of performing sludge mobilisation and cleaning operations while the tank remains operational. The system was designed to operate within confined tank environments while meeting strict safety and reliability requirements associated with hazardous industrial facilities.

Key Engineering Challenges

  • Operating within confined tank environments with limited access points
  • Designing systems capable of functioning in hazardous industrial environments
  • Handling complex sludge behaviour and fluid interactions
  • Ensuring reliability and maintainability for real industrial deployment
  • Integrating mechanical, hydraulic, and sensing systems into a compact robotic platform

Development Lifecycle

Feasibility → Concept Development → System & Detailed Design → Prototype Development → Testing & Validation → Field Deployment

The development of the system progressed through multiple engineering phases beginning with feasibility studies and concept exploration, followed by subsystem design, prototype development, testing, and field deployment. Each stage involved iterative refinement and validation to ensure reliable system performance under real operating conditions.

Key Engineering Areas

  • Mechanical system architecture and subsystem design
  • Hydraulic system integration and actuation mechanisms
  • Structural design and engineering analysis
  • Prototype development and design iteration
  • System integration and field validation

Engineering Contributions & Learnings

The DRACO project provided significant exposure to engineering challenges within the oil and gas industry, particularly in the context of hazardous industrial environments. Working on this system required understanding refinery operations, safety procedures, and the design considerations necessary for equipment operating in potentially explosive atmospheres.

The project also involved studying hazardous area equipment standards and understanding how mechanical and hydraulic systems must be designed to minimize ignition risks and ensure safe operation in environments where flammable vapours may be present.

  • Studied explosion protection requirements relevant to hazardous industrial environments, including ATEX Directive 2014/34/EU, PESO recommendations, and implementation considerations from IEC / IS 60079 and 80079 series standards for electrical and non-electrical equipment in explosive atmospheres.
  • Contributed to the overall mechanical architecture and subsystem layout of the robotic system, ensuring integration of propulsion, pumping, and hydraulic actuation mechanisms within deployment constraints.
  • Selected materials and manufacturing processes suitable for operation in crude oil environments and other demanding industrial conditions.
  • Designed the hydraulic circuit architecture and selected appropriate hydraulic components and configurations for the pumping and actuation systems, including components suitable for hazardous environments.
  • Performed engineering calculations to study propulsion mechanisms used for robotic manoeuvring, along with hydraulic system performance, component sizing, and system losses.
  • Conducted structural analysis and engineering evaluations to support mechanical design decisions during subsystem development.
  • Led the development of a scaled prototype of a piston-based pumping system used to study sludge mobilisation and evaluate system performance.
  • Designed and implemented an inductive proximity sensor–based position sensing approach for hydraulic cylinders to support automated piston pump operation.
  • Supported subsystem integration and assembly activities to ensure compatibility between mechanical, hydraulic, and sensing components during prototype development.
  • Refined mechanical designs through iterative testing and evaluation, incorporating insights from prototype trials and field validation activities.
  • Over time, I took on increasing responsibility and later led engineering activities across subsystem design, prototype development, testing, and field implementation while coordinating with cross-functional teams.

Testing & Validation

Multiple prototype iterations were developed and evaluated through controlled testing environments before field deployment. Testing activities focused on verifying system reliability, operational performance, and subsystem integration.

Dedicated in-house testing setups were also developed to simulate operational conditions and support iterative improvements to the system design prior to field implementation.

Project Outcome

The project resulted in the successful development of a robotic system designed to enable safer and more efficient crude oil tank cleaning operations while minimising operational disruption and reducing the need for human entry into hazardous environments.
